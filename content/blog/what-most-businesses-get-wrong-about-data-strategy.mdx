Every organisation with more than a dozen people and a CRM has a data strategy problem. The problem usually isn't that they don't have data — it's that they've confused having data with knowing what to do with it.

Here's the pattern we see most often when a new client comes to us for strategy work.

## The tooling trap

The first thing most teams do when they decide to "get serious about data" is buy a tool. A BI platform, a data warehouse, a machine learning service. The purchase feels like progress.

Six months later, the tool is underused, the data is still a mess, and the team is looking for the next tool to solve the problem the last tool didn't.

The issue is that tools amplify existing processes — they don't replace the need for clear thinking about what you're actually trying to know.

## Questions before infrastructure

A good data strategy starts with a much simpler question: what decisions do we currently make by guessing that we could make with data?

That question is harder than it sounds. It requires talking to the people who make decisions, not just the people who manage data. It requires being willing to hear that your most elaborate dashboard isn't answering anything useful.

When we do strategy engagements, we spend the first week doing nothing but this: understanding the decision landscape, mapping who needs what, and finding the places where better information would actually change behaviour.

## The build vs. buy trap

The second common mistake is at the opposite end: building everything bespoke when a standard tool would do fine.

Custom infrastructure has a carrying cost. It needs maintenance, documentation, and someone who understands it. Unless you have a genuinely unusual problem, the carrying cost usually outweighs the benefits.

The right answer is almost always: buy the commodity layers, build at the points of differentiation.

## What a good data strategy looks like

A good data strategy is short. It answers three questions:

1. What are the three most important decisions we need to make better?
2. What data do we need to make those decisions well?
3. What is the minimum viable infrastructure to get that data flowing reliably?

Everything else is a subsequent iteration.
